Introduction: A Country Built on Memory

The war in Bosnia and Herzegovina left deep scars in its society, visible through its urban fabric. As Yugoslavia disintegrated, long-standing multi-ethnic communities fractured along nationalist lines.

What followed was one of Europe's most devastating conflicts since World War II, with over 100,000 people killed, two million displaced and entire towns and cities destroyed. The war tore through everyday life, not only with violence, but by dismantling the delicate social fabric that once held diverse communities together.

Building in Mostar pockmarked with bullet holes, left unrepaired.

Nearly three decades later, Bosnia and Herzegovina remains a post-conflict society, grappling with how to confront and remember its traumatic past. Nowhere is this more palpable than in the urban environments of Sarajevo and Mostar. These two cities experienced some of the war's most visible destruction and now serve as open-air archives of its legacy. Buildings are still pockmarked by bullet holes and explosives, some even partially destroyed and left to decay. Monuments have also been erected in silence or controversy. The architecture in Bosnia and Herzegovina tells a complex story of survival, loss and contested memory.

This blog explores how architecture serves as a tool of remembrance in post-war Bosnia and Herzegovina. It draws on the framework of French historian Pierre Nora, who introduced the idea of lieux de m√©moire, or sites of memory. According to Nora, when societies can no longer rely on lived communal memory because of rupture, trauma or generational distance, they begin to embed memory in material forms such as buildings, ruins, memorials and symbolic spaces. In Bosnia and Herzegovina, narratives of the past remain politically sensitive and divided, hence the built-environment becomes the primary medium in which memory is preserved, negotiated or even suppressed and erased.

This journey explores how architecture serves as a tool of remembrance, drawing on Pierre Nora's concept of lieux de m√©moire - sites where memory crystallizes when living memory disappears.

The City as a Living Museum

Living Testimonies

In post-war Bosnia and Herzegovina, official narratives of the past remain divided and reconciliation remains difficult. Buildings that are damaged and left to decay often serve as the most honest witness to its violent history.

These structures do not speak through curated exhibitions or plaques at memorials, but through its physical material scars. Bullet holes, shattered windows, shell marks and even partially destroyed structures that are left untouched serve as living testimonies, silent yet powerful reminders of the war's physical and emotional toll etched into the everyday fabric of the city.

The Contested Landscape

Where architectural wounds remain open and reconstruction tells political stories

üåâ

A City Divided by Memory

In Mostar, architectural witnessing takes a different yet equally complex form. While the Stari Most (Old Bridge) has been faithfully reconstructed after its destruction in 1993, many residential and religious buildings on both sides of the Neretva River still show the scars of war.

This dichotomy can be felt through the urban fabric, with entire blocks remaining in disrepair, awkwardly restored, or conspicuously neglected. War-damaged buildings often sit beside freshly reconstructed religious or civic structures, reflecting how specific investment and memory politics can depict the priorities of their respective communities, as if caught in a tension between preservation and forgetting.

Case Study: Razvitak Department Store

A Symbol Frozen in Time

Pre-War

Once a symbol of modern urban life in Yugoslavia, the Razvitak department store now sits abandoned in the heart of the city, with its facade laden with bullet holes and its structure visibly decaying.

War Period

It used to feature a shopping centre with a multi-storey apartment above it, but due to heavy bombing and shelling, the apartment had to be demolished, while the shopping centre was left to decay.

Present Day

Furthermore, the shopping centre is located near the invisible dividing line between the Croat majority west and Bosniak majority east. The building's neglect is not merely due to economics or disrepair, it is a spatial embodiment of contested memory.

‚öñÔ∏è

Rebuilding Razvitak would require cooperation, agreement and a shared vision of the future between the Croats and Bosniak politicians, where even schools and public monuments are highly contested, often duplicated by ethnicity.

The Silent Witness

As such, the building remains frozen in time, a hollow witness to what Mostar once was and what it still struggles to become. Its silence is louder than any memorial plaque, a visible void where shared urban life used to exist, now avoided and ignored.

The Periphery's Perspective

02

Architecture as Witness

Even places that escaped direct violence bear witness to larger historical traumas

Budva offered an unexpected entry point into post-conflict reconstruction. Unlike Sarajevo or Mostar, this ancient coastal town had largely escaped the direct violence of the 1990s wars.

Yet walking through its medieval old town and along its modern waterfront revealed how even peripheral locations become witnesses to larger historical traumas.

üì∑

Budva Old Town

Medieval walls and Venetian architecture preserved through conflict

"The old town of Budva, enclosed within Venetian-era walls, presented itself as a preserved fragment of pre-war Yugoslavia - a glimpse of what might have been preserved elsewhere."

The narrow stone streets, Orthodox churches alongside Catholic ones, and the easy mingling of different architectural traditions offered a vision of the multicultural coexistence that had characterized much of Yugoslavia.

But even in this relatively peaceful setting, subtle traces of political transformation emerged. Yugoslav-era hotels now bearing international chain names, communist-era apartment blocks slowly being renovated or replaced - all representing both material legacy and ongoing post-socialist transformation.

Architecture for Reconciliation

03

Subtle Scars, Quiet Healing

How a city under siege for 1,425 days rebuilds trust through everyday architecture

In Sarajevo, architectural witnessing reveals itself not through dramatic ruins, but through subtly repaired scars. The longest siege in modern European history left marks that speak quietly through mismatched materials and visible repairs.

Buildings across the city, especially in neighborhoods like Grbavica and along former frontlines, show signs of quick post-war repairs. Walls once perforated by bullets have been patched up, windows replaced, facades re-rendered.

üîç

Architectural Detective Work

Yet the outlines of original damage remain faintly visible beneath fresh plaster or paint. Close examination reveals inconsistencies in texture, mismatched materials, and outlines of patchwork over old bullet holes. These traces betray what repaired surfaces try to forget.

üì∑

Patched Facades

Subtle repair work visible in Grbavica neighborhood

"Even buildings that have been 'healed' still function as witnesses, holding the residue of trauma on their walls. Memory persists not through declared memorials, but through material traces that endure beneath paint, plaster and time."

Along the streets of Sarajevo, the desire to move on meets architecture's quiet refusal to forget. This tension between erasure and endurance, between rebuilding and the impossibility of complete repair, creates a unique form of memory preservation.

Everyday Reconciliation

But Sarajevo also demonstrates how architecture can actively foster reconciliation. New community centers, restored cultural institutions, and shared public spaces create opportunities for former enemies to interact in neutral settings.

The restoration of the National Library, the reconstruction of religious buildings, and the careful preservation of Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, and Yugoslav architectural layers tell a story of a city learning to honor its complex heritage.

Final Reflections

Personal conclusions on architecture, memory, and reconciliation

The buildings of the former Yugoslavia continue to shape how the past is remembered and how the future might be envisioned. In their bullet holes and careful reconstructions, their abandonment and renewal, they offer lessons for any society learning to live with the scars of conflict.

The journey continues - in every repaired wall, every preserved ruin, every shared public space where former enemies learn to coexist. Architecture, it turns out, is one of our most powerful tools for both remembering and healing.
